Hype or not? Formalizing Automatic Promotional Language Detection in Biomedical Research

Bojan Batalo1, Erica K. Shimomoto1, Dipesh Satav2, Neil Millar2

Abstract

In science, promotional language ("hype") is increasing and can undermine objective evaluation of evidence, impede research development, and erode trust in science. We introduce the task of automatic detection of hype, defined as hyperbolic or subjective language that authors use to glamorize, promote, embellish, or exaggerate aspects of their research.

We propose formalized guidelines for identifying hype language and apply them to annotate a portion of the National Institutes of Health (NIH) grant application corpus. The annotation process revealed fair inter-annotator agreement initially, which improved substantially after discussion and clarification of guidelines (Cohen's Kappa: 0.30–0.52 before, 0.94–0.96 after).

We evaluate traditional text classifiers and language models on this task, comparing their performance with a human baseline. Our experiments show that formalizing annotation guidelines can help humans reliably annotate candidate hype adjectives and that using our annotated dataset to train machine learning models yields promising results. Our annotation guidelines and dataset are available at https://github.com/hype-busters/eacl2026-hype-dataset.

The Problem: Hype in Scientific Publishing

Promotional language in scientific writing has been increasing in biomedical funding applications and journal publications. Terms such as revolutionary, unprecedented, groundbreaking, and transformative are becoming more common, yet they are rarely justified and can:

  • Undermine Objectivity: Words like "groundbreaking" and "transformative" impose value judgments rather than letting research speak for itself
  • Bias Readers:
    • Promotional language can influence readers' evaluation of research quality beyond the actual evidence presented
  • Erode Trust: When promotional language creates unrealistic expectations or misrepresents findings, public trust in science is eroded
  • Impede Development: Overstatement can misdirect research efforts and policy decisions

While promotional language is common, determining whether a specific term constitutes hype remains problematic. The same word may be promotional in one context but neutral or technical in another. For example, the adjectives essential and meticulous can promote significance or rigor ("meticulous experimental design"), but they may also occur in neutral contexts or as part of technical terms ("essential fatty acid", "meticulous hemostasis").

Proposed Annotation Guidelines

To address the subjectivity inherent in hype detection, we propose six sequential annotation steps that guide annotators through a systematic evaluation process:

Step 1: Value-Judgment

Does the adjective imply positive value judgment? Most selected adjectives do, including priority claims ("first method to..."). If yes, proceed to steps 2-6. If not, mark as Not Hype.

Step 2: Hyperbolic

Is the adjective hyperbolic or exaggerated? Examples: revolutionary, unprecedented, unparalleled, groundbreaking. These are usually promotional: "unparalleled opportunity", "revolutionary tool".

Step 3: Gratuitous

Can the adjective be removed without loss of meaning? If the propositional content remains unchanged or the adjective is redundant ("discovered a novel gene"), mark as Hype. If removal substantially alters meaning or justification is given, mark as Not Hype.

Step 4: Amplified

Is the adjective strengthened by modifiers? Examples: "truly novel", "extremely rigorous", "highly cohesive".

Step 5: Coordinated

Is the adjective coordinated with other hype candidates (adjective stacking)? Example: "innovative and strong researcher".

Step 6: Broader Context

When ambiguous, consider the overall tone. If the sentence contains multiple promotional adjectives, mark as Hype.

Discussion sessions were held to resolve conflicts between annotators. These discussions resolved 545 out of 575 initial disagreements, significantly improving inter-annotator agreement from Cohen's Kappa of 0.30–0.52 to 0.94–0.96.

NIH Grant Application Dataset

We annotated 1,270 sentences from the NIH grant application abstract corpus, focusing on novelty and rigour adjective groups:

Dataset Statistics:

  • Total Sentences: 1,270 annotated sentences
  • Hype Examples: 917 (72.2%)
  • Not Hype Examples: 353 (27.8%)
  • Novelty Adjectives: 11 members (creative, emerging, first, groundbreaking, innovative, latest, novel, revolutionary, unique, unparalleled, unprecedented)
  • Rigour Adjectives: 15 members (accurate, advanced, careful, cohesive, detailed, nuanced, powerful, quality, reproducible, rigorous, robust, scientific, sophisticated, strong, systematic)

Most Promotional Adjectives (100% classified as Hype): groundbreaking, revolutionary, unparalleled, unprecedented, careful

Classification Rationales:

  • Gratuitous (683 samples): Adjectives adding little substance to the proposition
  • Broader Context (220 samples): Hype determined by overall sentence tone
  • Hyperbolic (208 samples): Exaggerated or unambiguous promotional adjectives
  • Coordinated (138 samples): Adjectives stacked with other hype candidates
  • Amplified (22 samples): Adjectives strengthened by modifiers

Experimental Results

We evaluated four categories of models: traditional text classifiers, pre-trained language models (PLMs), large language models (LLMs), and human annotators.

Traditional Text Classifiers

Methods based on bag-of-words and GLOVE embeddings showed limited performance. SVM achieved the best accuracy (77.6%) but lagged behind the human baseline due to bias toward classifying sentences as Hype for adjectives like "groundbreaking" and "revolutionary".

Pre-trained Language Models

Fine-tuned BERT-based models substantially outperformed both traditional classifiers and the human baseline:

  • BERT + Finetuning: 86.2% accuracy, 0.909 F1-score, 0.812 macro F1-score
  • DISTILBERT + Finetuning: 85.8% accuracy (best macro F1 of 0.821)
  • GPT-2 + Finetuning: 80.7% accuracy
  • BIOMEDBERT + Finetuning: 83.5% accuracy (domain knowledge did not improve performance)

Bidirectional models (BERT-based) outperformed causal models (GPT-2) because context appears both before and after the adjective.

Large Language Models

Zero-shot LLMs underperformed:

  • LLAMA3.1-INST. (+Broad Prompt): 45.1% accuracy (worse than traditional methods)
  • GPT-4O-MINI (+Broad Prompt): 63.1% accuracy (below human baseline)
  • GPT-4O-MINI (+Strict Prompt): 67.5% accuracy (improved with stricter definition)

LLMs showed bias toward classifying as "Not Hype" due to exposure to common academic parlance in training data.

Human Baseline

Two researchers with 10+ years academic experience achieved 76.7% accuracy and 0.730 macro F1-score on the hold-out test set using only the broad definition of hype (without guidelines). This validates the complexity of the task and the value of formal guidelines.

Key Findings

  • Formalized Guidelines Help: Annotation guidelines substantially improved inter-annotator agreement and human baseline performance
  • Hype Detection is Context-Dependent: The same adjective (e.g., "essential", "nuanced") can be hype or neutral depending on context
  • Fine-tuned Models Excel: Fine-tuned BERT models achieved 86.2% accuracy, surpassing both traditional methods and zero-shot LLMs
  • Domain Knowledge Matters: Domain-specific knowledge was important for both annotators and models, especially for rigour adjectives
  • Adjective-Specific Challenges: Some adjectives (emerging, latest, scientific, nuanced) were consistently difficult to classify, even for humans
  • Most Hype from Gratuitous Use: The largest category of promotional adjectives (683 samples) is gratuitous use—adjectives that can be removed without loss of meaning

Applications & Future Directions

Applications:

  • Automated Editing Tools: Flagging promotional language to help authors write more objectively
  • Scientific Fact-Checking: Foundation for downstream fact-checking systems
  • Publication Quality: Assisting editors in identifying over-promoted claims
  • Research Transparency: Supporting more objective communication in grant applications and publications

Future Work:

  • Develop guidelines for the remaining six adjective groups (importance, scale, utility, quality, attitude, problem)
  • Expand lexicon to include adverbs, nouns, and verbs
  • Consider intensity levels of hype (not just binary classification)
  • Apply to other research fields and languages
  • Scale annotation process to larger datasets
