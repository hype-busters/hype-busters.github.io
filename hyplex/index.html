Best-Worst Scaling of Hype in Biomedical Research: Building an Intensity Lexicon of Promotional Adjectives

Neil Millar1, Dipesh Satav1, Bojan Batalo2, Erica K. Shimomoto2, Ryosuke L. Ohniwa1
1University of Tsukuba, 2National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology (AIST)
LREC-COLING 2025
Paper BWS Platform GitHub Page

Hyplex is an intensity-scaled lexicon of 303 promotional adjectives attested in biomedical writing across eight evaluative domains (e.g. IMPORTANCE, NOVELTY, RIGOUR), developed using Best-Worst Scaling with human ratings.

Abstract

Promotional language, or "hype", is increasingly common in biomedical research reporting. Adjectives such as groundbreaking, robust, and impactful can engage readers but also risk imposing value judgements and undermining objectivity. Detecting and assessing such language requires distinguishing degrees of promotional intensity (e.g., new < novel < groundbreaking < revolutionary), yet no such graded resource exists.

We present an intensity-scaled lexicon of 303 promotional adjectives attested in biomedical writing across eight evaluative domains (e.g. IMPORTANCE, NOVELTY, RIGOUR). Ratings were obtained through Best–Worst Scaling (BWS) with human participants evaluating adjectives for promotional strength in the context of scientific research reporting. We refer to this as the Hyplex resource (Hype Lexicon).

The ratings show high internal consistency (r = 0.87; 95% CI [0.85, 0.89]) and correlate most strongly with arousal and dominance in the NRC VAD Lexicon, suggesting that promotional intensity aligns more with reader activation and perceptions of assertiveness than simple positivity. We also release an online BWS platform integrated with the R package bwsTools to support intensity-scaling research in other domains.

Method Overview

Best-Worst Scaling Annotation Process. We applied Best–Worst Scaling (BWS) to obtain promotional intensity ratings for 303 adjectives common in biomedical texts. Participants were shown sets of four adjectives and asked to select the most promotional ("best") and least promotional ("worst") term in each set. The adjectives fall into eight semantic categories: IMPORTANCE (imperative, paramount), NOVELTY (revolutionary, ground-breaking), SCALE (massive, vast), RIGOUR (careful, sophisticated), UTILITY (impactful, seamless), QUALITIES (renowned, stellar), ATTITUDE (incredible, excellent), and PROBLEM (dire, devastating).

Annotation followed a Balanced Incomplete Block Design (BIBD) constructed separately for each semantic category using the bwsTools package in R. Ten annotators (including native speakers and expert English users) completed surveys over five days using our custom BWS platform. Final promotional intensity scores were calculated using difference-scoring and linearly calibrated using embedded anchor items (high, neutral, and low) to ensure consistency across categories.

Key Results

The lexicon demonstrated high internal consistency with an overall split-half reliability of r = 0.87 (95% CI [0.85, 0.89]). Category-specific reliability ranged from r = 0.75 (RIGOUR) to r = 0.94 (ATTITUDE). Categories with clearer evaluative meanings showed higher consistency, while more technical categories (UTILITY, RIGOUR) showed more individual variation.

The distribution of promotional intensity scores was similar across categories, with means ranging from 0.55 (PROBLEM) to 0.64 (SCALE). High-intensity examples include: extraordinary, revolutionary, catastrophic, flawless, colossal. Low-intensity examples include: intriguing, fresh, unanswered, subtle, ample.

Comparison with the NRC VAD Lexicon revealed that promotional intensity correlates most strongly with arousal (r = 0.37) and dominance (r = 0.36) dimensions, but weakly with valence (r = 0.11). This suggests promotional language aims to activate readers and convey assertiveness rather than simple positivity.

Applications

The Hyplex resource enables various research applications:

  • NLP Features: Providing interpretable features for hype-detection systems and developing hype-aware embeddings
  • Benchmarking: Serving as gold-standard reference for evaluating automatic promotionality detection methods
  • Diachronic Analysis: Tracking changes in promotional language across time, domains, or publication types
  • Research Impact: Examining whether promotional intensity influences funding success, publication, and citation impact
  • Resource Extension: Using the BWS platform to crowdsource additional annotations and expand coverage
